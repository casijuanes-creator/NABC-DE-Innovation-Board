## Overview

Disciplined Entrepreneurship (DE), created by Bill Aulet at MIT, provides a systematic 24-step process for building a successful new venture. The framework is built on a core premise: **entrepreneurship can be taught and practiced as a discipline, not just an art**. While passion and creativity matter, the difference between success and failure is usually rigor and process.

The framework begins with a foundational step â€” **Step 0: Define Your Raison d'ÃŠtre** â€” which establishes why this venture should exist and why you are the right person to build it. This anchors everything that follows.

The 24 steps are then organized into six major themes that answer the fundamental questions every new venture must resolve:

1. **Who is your customer?** (Steps 1â€“6)
2. **What can you do for your customer?** (Steps 7â€“11)
3. **How does your customer acquire your product?** (Steps 12â€“14)
4. **How do you make money off your product?** (Steps 15â€“19)
5. **How do you design and build your product?** (Steps 20â€“22)
6. **How do you scale your business?** (Steps 23â€“24)

### Key Principles

**Entrepreneurship is not linear.** While the steps are numbered, real ventures iterate. You will revisit earlier steps as you learn. The numbering provides logical dependency, not a rigid sequence.

**Start with the customer, not the technology.** Many founders start with what they can build rather than what someone needs. DE insists on deep customer understanding before committing to a solution.

**Beachhead first.** Rather than pursuing a broad market, DE requires selecting one narrow market segment â€” the beachhead â€” and dominating it before expanding. This focus is counterintuitive but critical.

**Quantify relentlessly.** Every step demands specificity. Not "a big market" but "42,000 potential customers spending $3.2B annually." Not "they'll love it" but "they'll save 14 hours per week, worth $2,100 at their billing rate."

## Step 0: Define Your Raison d'ÃŠtre (The Foundation)

**Purpose:** Before any market analysis or customer discovery, articulate WHY this venture should exist â€” what is the founding vision, passion, and unique insight that drives everything that follows?

**What you produce:** A clear, compelling statement of your venture's reason for being â€” the intersection of what you're passionate about, what you're uniquely good at, and what the world needs.

**Why this comes first:**

Aulet emphasizes in the Workbook and in his MIT teaching that the Raison d'ÃŠtre is the anchor for the entire 24-step journey. Without it, entrepreneurs risk:
- Pivoting into opportunities they don't care about (and will abandon when things get hard)
- Pursuing ideas where they have no unique right to win
- Losing motivation when the inevitable difficulties arrive
- Building a business that succeeds financially but feels hollow

The Raison d'ÃŠtre is NOT the same as the business idea. It's deeper. It's the reason YOU are the right person (or team) to pursue THIS problem space. The specific business idea, beachhead, and product will evolve through the 24 steps â€” but the Raison d'ÃŠtre should remain stable.

**How to do it:**

The Raison d'ÃŠtre sits at the intersection of three elements:

1. **What are you passionate about?**
   - What problems keep you up at night?
   - What topics do you read about, think about, and talk about voluntarily?
   - What work have you done that felt most meaningful?
   - If money were no object, what would you work on?

2. **What are you uniquely good at?**
   - What skills, knowledge, or capabilities do you (and your team) have that others don't?
   - What's your "unfair advantage" â€” domain expertise, relationships, technical capability, proprietary insight?
   - What experiences have given you a perspective that others lack?
   - What can you do that would take a competitor years to replicate?

3. **What does the world need?**
   - What important problems are not being solved well?
   - Where is there significant pain, waste, or unmet potential?
   - What changes in technology, regulation, or society create new opportunities?
   - Where is the gap between what exists and what should exist?

**The sweet spot** â€” your Raison d'ÃŠtre â€” is where all three overlap. If you're passionate but not skilled, you'll be outcompeted. If you're skilled but not passionate, you'll burn out. If you're passionate and skilled but the world doesn't need it, you'll build something nobody wants.

**Raison d'ÃŠtre statement format:**

*"We exist to [solve this problem / create this change] for [these people / this domain] because [our unique insight / capability / experience] puts us in a position that few others occupy, and we are deeply committed to this mission because [personal connection / passion / belief]."*

**Examples:**
- "We exist to make radiology diagnosis 10x faster because our team spent 15 years in hospital radiology and we've built AI models that no academic lab has access to, and we're driven by having seen too many patients wait days for results that should take minutes."
- "We exist to democratize financial planning for immigrants because our founder navigated this broken system personally, we understand the cultural and linguistic barriers that fintech ignores, and we believe financial literacy shouldn't depend on your country of origin."

**How the Raison d'ÃŠtre connects to everything that follows:**
- **Steps 1â€“2** (Segmentation & Beachhead): Your Raison d'ÃŠtre constrains which markets you should even consider. If a segment doesn't connect to your passion and capability, it's not your market â€” even if it's lucrative.
- **Step 10** (Define Your Core): Your core differentiator should be rooted in your Raison d'ÃŠtre. The unique insight that drives the venture should manifest as a unique competitive advantage.
- **Step 19** (Key Assumptions): One of your most important assumptions is that your team's passion and capability are sufficient. The Raison d'ÃŠtre makes this assumption explicit.
- **Step 24** (Business Plan): Investors and stakeholders want to know WHY you. The Raison d'ÃŠtre is the answer.

**For existing ideas being reviewed:** Even if you're evaluating an existing product or business, asking "What's the Raison d'ÃŠtre?" is revealing. If the founding team can't articulate it clearly, or if the current direction has drifted away from it, that's a diagnostic signal.

**ðŸ”„ NABC Connection:** The Raison d'ÃŠtre feeds directly into every component of NABC:
- **Need**: Your passion points you toward needs you understand deeply
- **Approach**: Your unique capabilities shape a differentiated approach
- **Benefits per Costs**: Your commitment drives the relentless iteration needed to achieve 2â€“10x
- **Competition**: Your Raison d'ÃŠtre IS the answer to "Why you? Why now?"

**Common mistakes:**
- Skipping this step because it feels "soft" (it's the most strategic step â€” it constrains everything)
- Confusing the Raison d'ÃŠtre with the product idea (it's deeper than any specific product)
- Being too generic ("We want to make the world better with AI")
- Not being honest about whether you actually have a unique capability
- Ignoring the passion dimension and optimizing only for market opportunity

- **v_Steve**: "Are you making a dent in the universe, or building a commodity?"

## Theme 1: Who Is Your Customer?

### Step 1: Market Segmentation

**Purpose:** Brainstorm and organize all the potential markets and applications for your idea into a structured list of distinct market opportunities.

**What you produce:** A segmentation matrix with 6â€“12 potential market opportunities, each defined by a specific end user, application, and context.

**How to do it:**
- Start by brainstorming broadly. List every possible customer type, industry, use case, and application you can imagine for your idea or technology.
- Group these into discrete market segments. Each segment should have a distinct end user performing a distinct task in a distinct context.
- For each segment, note: Who is the end user? What are they using it for? What's their biggest pain or gain? How big is the opportunity? How well does your idea fit?
- Resist the urge to pick a winner immediately. The goal is a wide, honest scan before narrowing.

**Key criteria for each segment:**
- Is the end user well-funded and reachable?
- Is the need urgent and important (not just "nice to have")?
- Can you deliver a complete solution to them?
- Is there an existing budget for this type of solution?
- Are there strong word-of-mouth dynamics within the segment?
- Can you leverage this segment to enter adjacent markets later?

**Common mistakes:**
- Defining segments too broadly (e.g., "healthcare" instead of "hospital radiology departments with >200 beds")
- Only listing segments where you already have connections
- Ignoring segments that seem small but could be ideal beachheads
- Confusing the buyer with the end user

**ðŸŽ¯ Board members most relevant:**
- **v_Indra**: Portfolio thinking â€” evaluating multiple segments as a portfolio of options
- **v_Jeff**: Customer obsession â€” which segment has the most desperate, underserved customer?
- **v_Warren**: Business fundamentals â€” which segments have the best unit economics potential?

### Step 2: Select a Beachhead Market

**Purpose:** Choose one â€” and only one â€” market segment to focus all your initial efforts on.

**What you produce:** A single, well-defined beachhead market with a clear rationale for why it comes first.

**How to do it:**
- Evaluate each segment from Step 1 against selection criteria (below).
- Use a scoring matrix if helpful, but don't let the scoring override judgment â€” the criteria are not equally weighted.
- The ideal beachhead is a market where: customers have money and urgency, you can deliver a complete product, the segment is small enough to dominate quickly, and winning it creates a platform for expansion into adjacent markets.

**Selection criteria (Aulet's priorities):**
1. **Does the target customer have money?** Can they pay, and do they have budget authority?
2. **Is the target customer readily accessible to your sales force?** Can you actually reach them?
3. **Does the target customer have a compelling reason to buy?** Is the pain acute enough to drive action?
4. **Can you deliver a whole product?** Not just a feature â€” a complete solution they can use today.
5. **Is there entrenched competition?** Less is better for a beachhead.
6. **Can you leverage this segment to enter adjacent ones?** The beachhead should be a strategic springboard.
7. **Is this consistent with your values, passions, and goals?** You'll spend years on this â€” it must matter to you.

**Why one market?** Startups die from indigestion, not starvation. Trying to serve multiple markets simultaneously fragments your resources, dilutes your product, confuses your messaging, and ensures you're mediocre everywhere instead of dominant somewhere.

**Common mistakes:**
- Picking the biggest market instead of the most winnable one
- Choosing based on familiarity rather than strategic fit
- Refusing to commit â€” keeping 2â€“3 segments "active"
- Picking a segment that doesn't lead anywhere (dead-end beachhead)

**ðŸ”„ NABC Checkpoint:** After selecting your beachhead, run a quick NABC to test whether you can articulate a clear Need for this specific segment. If the Need is vague, your beachhead selection may be wrong.

**ðŸŽ¯ Board members most relevant:**
- **v_SunTzu**: Strategic positioning â€” where can you win with current resources?
- **v_Steve**: Focus â€” the discipline to say no to attractive but distracting markets
- **v_Ben**: Reality check â€” can you actually dominate this segment, or are you being optimistic?
- **v_Andy**: Strategic inflection â€” is this market at a 10x change moment?

### Step 3: Build an End User Profile

**Purpose:** Create a detailed, specific description of the end user within your beachhead market â€” the actual human who will use your product.

**What you produce:** A richly detailed end user profile (not a marketing persona â€” a real, specific description).

**How to do it:**
- Identify a real person (or composite of 2â€“3 real people) who represents your target end user. If you haven't talked to real end users yet, this step is your signal to go do customer discovery.
- Describe their demographics, but go much deeper: their day, their frustrations, their goals, their constraints, their decision-making patterns, what they read, who they trust, what motivates them.
- Include both functional characteristics (job title, budget authority, technical skill) and psychographic ones (risk tolerance, openness to new solutions, status consciousness).
- The profile should be specific enough that you could walk into their workplace and recognize them.

**Key elements of the profile:**
- **Demographics**: Age range, gender, education, income, location
- **Professional context**: Title, responsibilities, reporting structure, KPIs
- **Day in their life**: What does a typical day look like? Where does the pain you're solving show up?
- **Goals and aspirations**: What are they trying to achieve? What does success look like for them?
- **Biggest frustrations**: What keeps them up at night? What takes too long? What's unreliable?
- **Current behavior**: How do they currently address the need you've identified? What tools/processes do they use?
- **Decision-making**: How do they evaluate and adopt new solutions? Who influences them?
- **Watering holes**: Where do they get information? Conferences, publications, online communities, peers?

**Common mistakes:**
- Building the profile from assumptions instead of real interviews
- Making it too generic ("marketing managers aged 30â€“45")
- Ignoring the emotional and social dimensions of their experience
- Not distinguishing between the end user and the economic buyer (they're often different people)

**ðŸŽ¯ Board members most relevant:**
- **v_Jeff**: Working backwards from the customer â€” radical specificity about who they are
- **v_Satya**: Empathy â€” truly understanding the user's inner world
- **v_Hitendra**: Inner alignment â€” understanding what motivates the user at a human level

### Step 4: Calculate the Total Addressable Market (TAM) for the Beachhead

**Purpose:** Determine the annual revenue opportunity if you achieved 100% market share in your beachhead market.

**What you produce:** A bottom-up TAM calculation with clear assumptions and data sources.

**How to do it:**
- **Always use bottom-up analysis.** Top-down TAM (e.g., "the healthcare IT market is $50B, we'll get 1%") is nearly useless. Instead, count the actual number of potential end users in your beachhead and multiply by what each would pay annually.
- Formula: **TAM = (Number of end users in beachhead) Ã— (Revenue per end user per year)**
- Both numbers require research: industry databases, government statistics, trade associations, LinkedIn searches, conference attendee lists, direct outreach.
- Be conservative. If you're uncertain about the number of end users, use the low estimate.

**TAM sizing guidance:**
- **Too small** (< $5M): May not justify the effort unless it's a springboard to much larger adjacent markets
- **Sweet spot** ($5Mâ€“$100M): Large enough to build a real business, small enough that incumbents don't care (yet)
- **Too large** (> $1B): You probably haven't segmented narrowly enough â€” your beachhead is too broad

**Important:** The beachhead TAM is NOT your company's total opportunity. It's your initial market. Your company's ultimate opportunity includes follow-on markets (Step 14). Investors and board members need to understand both numbers.

**Common mistakes:**
- Using top-down analysis ("the market is $X billion")
- Confusing beachhead TAM with total company opportunity
- Not verifying assumptions with real data
- Double-counting (e.g., counting both the end user and their company)

**ðŸŽ¯ Board members most relevant:**
- **v_Warren**: Business fundamentals â€” are the economics real?
- **v_Elon**: First principles â€” do the numbers survive scrutiny from basic physics/math?
- **v_Indra**: Market dynamics â€” what forces could grow or shrink this TAM?

### Step 5: Profile the Persona for the Beachhead Market

**Purpose:** Narrow the end user profile (Step 3) into a single, specific persona â€” one real person who embodies your target customer.

**What you produce:** A named, detailed persona with a photo (or illustration), demographic details, behavioral patterns, priorities, and decision-making criteria.

**How to do it:**
- From your end user research, identify ONE real individual who best represents your beachhead customer. Give them a name (use their real name or a pseudonym).
- This is not a composite â€” it's a single person. You should be able to call this person on the phone.
- Build out their profile in extreme detail: What do they care about most? What keeps them from solving this problem today? What would make them switch to your solution? What would make them resist?
- Use this persona as a decision-making filter for everything: "Would [Persona Name] want this feature? Would [Persona Name] pay this price? Would [Persona Name] find this through [channel]?"

**The persona should include:**
- Name and photo
- Key demographics and professional details
- Their priority ranking of needs/wants
- The "watering holes" where they find information
- Their biggest fear about adopting a new solution
- What success looks like for them (in their words)
- Their typical evaluation and buying process

**Why a single person?** Because specificity drives better decisions. "We're building for Sarah, the head of radiology at Mass General, who needs to reduce read times by 40% to meet new volume targets" drives infinitely better product decisions than "we're building for radiologists."

**Common mistakes:**
- Making the persona fictional without basing it on real interviews
- Creating multiple personas (you should have ONE for your beachhead)
- Making it too polished â€” real personas have contradictions and complexity
- Not updating the persona as you learn more

**ðŸ”„ NABC Checkpoint:** With your persona defined, revisit your NABC. Can you articulate the Need in terms this specific person would immediately recognize? Can you describe the Benefit in their language, using their metrics of success?

**ðŸŽ¯ Board members most relevant:**
- **v_Steve**: "Would this person love this? Not like it â€” LOVE it?"
- **v_Jeff**: "What would make this person say 'I can't live without this'?"
- **v_Hitendra**: Understanding the full human behind the professional role

### Step 6: Full Life Cycle Use Case

**Purpose:** Map the complete journey of how your persona discovers, evaluates, acquires, uses, pays for, and gets value from your product.

**What you produce:** A step-by-step narrative of the persona's entire experience with your product, from first awareness through ongoing use and advocacy.

**How to do it:**
- Walk through the persona's journey chronologically. Start from before they know you exist and go all the way through to being a loyal, recommending customer.
- At each stage, identify: What triggers the transition? What barriers exist? What emotions are present? What alternatives are they considering?
- Be brutally honest about friction points. Where will the persona get stuck? Where might they drop off?

**Life cycle stages to map:**
1. **Awareness**: How does the persona first learn about the problem and your solution?
2. **Evaluation**: How do they assess whether your solution is right for them?
3. **Acquisition**: How do they purchase/sign up? What's the process?
4. **Installation/Setup**: How do they get started? How long does this take?
5. **First use**: What's the initial experience? Do they get value immediately?
6. **Ongoing use**: What does regular usage look like? How often? How deeply?
7. **Value realization**: When do they first feel "this was worth it"? How long does that take?
8. **Support/maintenance**: What happens when things go wrong?
9. **Expansion**: Do they use it for more things over time? Do they bring others?
10. **Advocacy**: Do they recommend it? To whom? Through what channels?

**Common mistakes:**
- Only mapping the "happy path" â€” ignoring where things go wrong
- Skipping the pre-purchase stages (awareness and evaluation are critical)
- Assuming frictionless adoption (there's always friction)
- Not distinguishing between what you WANT the journey to be and what it WILL be

**ðŸŽ¯ Board members most relevant:**
- **v_Steve**: End-to-end experience design â€” "Where does the magic happen? Where does it break?"
- **v_Meg**: Operational reality â€” "Can you actually deliver this experience at scale?"
- **v_Satya**: Platform thinking â€” "How does this experience create a platform for growth?"

## Theme 2: What Can You Do for Your Customer?

### Step 7: High-Level Product Specification

**Purpose:** Define what your product is at a high level â€” features, functionality, and form â€” based on what your persona needs, NOT what you can build.

**What you produce:** A product specification document that describes the product from the customer's perspective, organized around their priorities.

**How to do it:**
- Start with the persona (Step 5) and the full life cycle (Step 6). What does the product need to do at each stage?
- Prioritize ruthlessly. Use the persona's priority ranking. The features that matter most to THEM go first â€” not the features that are most interesting to build.
- Describe functionality in customer language, not technical language. "Generates a compliance report in one click" not "automated PDF rendering with data aggregation pipeline."
- Include a visual â€” even a rough sketch or wireframe. Visual representation forces clarity that words alone cannot.

**Product specification should include:**
- **Core functionality**: The 3â€“5 things the product MUST do to deliver its value proposition
- **Supporting functionality**: Additional features that enhance the core
- **Out of scope**: Explicitly list what the product does NOT do (this is as important as what it does)
- **Brochure test**: Could you create a one-page brochure from this spec that would make the persona excited?

**Common mistakes:**
- Feature creep â€” listing everything you could build instead of what the persona needs
- Technical focus â€” describing architecture instead of user experience
- No prioritization â€” treating all features as equally important
- Skipping the visual â€” words are insufficient for product definition

**ðŸŽ¯ Board members most relevant:**
- **v_Steve**: Ruthless simplification â€” "What can we remove and make it better?"
- **v_Jeff**: Working backwards â€” "Does this brochure make the customer excited?"
- **v_Elon**: First principles â€” "What's the simplest version that delivers the core value?"

### Step 8: Quantify the Value Proposition

**Purpose:** Assign specific, measurable numbers to the value your product creates for the persona.

**What you produce:** A quantified value proposition that expresses benefits in the persona's metrics, with a clear comparison to the status quo.

**How to do it:**
- Identify the 1â€“3 most important metrics for your persona. These come directly from their priorities (Step 5) and the life cycle (Step 6).
- Quantify the "as-is" state: How much time/money/effort does the persona currently spend? What outcomes do they currently get?
- Quantify the "to-be" state: With your product, what changes? By how much?
- Express value as a ratio or improvement factor: "3x faster," "$4,200 saved per month," "reduces errors from 12% to 0.5%."

**The quantification must be:**
- **Specific**: Numbers, not adjectives
- **Credible**: Based on real data or defensible estimates, not wishes
- **Meaningful to the persona**: In THEIR metrics, not yours
- **Significant**: Aim for 2â€“10x improvement (Carlson's NABC threshold). Marginal improvements don't motivate behavior change.

**Value dimensions to quantify:**
- **Time**: Hours/days saved per week/month/year
- **Money**: Direct cost savings or revenue increase
- **Quality**: Error rates, defect rates, satisfaction scores
- **Risk**: Reduction in probability or impact of negative events
- **Convenience**: Steps eliminated, complexity reduced
- **Emotional**: Status, confidence, peace of mind (harder to quantify but real)

**ðŸ”„ NABC Checkpoint:** This step IS the Benefits per Costs component of NABC. Run a full NABC review here. Your quantified value proposition should make the Benefits per Costs section compelling and specific. If it doesn't, you need more customer data.

**Common mistakes:**
- Vague claims ("saves time" instead of "saves 14 hours per week")
- Only quantifying one dimension (usually time or money) while ignoring others
- Not anchoring to the persona's current situation (the "as-is" baseline)
- Overstating benefits without evidence

**ðŸŽ¯ Board members most relevant:**
- **v_Warren**: "Show me the math. Is this value real and sustainable?"
- **v_Ben**: "Have you validated these numbers with actual customers, or are you making them up?"
- **v_Elon**: "Can we make this 10x, not 2x? What would need to be true?"

### Step 9: Identify Your Next 10 Customers

**Purpose:** Validate that your beachhead market is real by identifying 10 specific, named potential customers beyond your persona.

**What you produce:** A list of 10 named potential customers (companies or individuals) with contact information and a brief note on why each is a fit.

**How to do it:**
- Using the persona profile (Step 5) as your filter, find 10 real entities that match.
- These should NOT be friends, family, or people who will say yes to be nice. They should be real potential customers who fit the beachhead profile.
- For each, note: Why do they fit the persona profile? What evidence suggests they have the need? How accessible are they? What's their likely budget?
- If you can't find 10, your beachhead might be too narrow or non-existent. If you easily find 100+, you might not be segmented enough.

**Why 10?** It's large enough to test whether the segment is real, but small enough that you can know each one personally. These become your early design partners, beta testers, and first paying customers.

**Common mistakes:**
- Listing organizations instead of specific people within them
- Including "nice to have" customers instead of "must have" ones
- Not actually contacting them (this step requires outreach, not just research)
- Giving up if the first few say no (iterate the approach, not the segment)

**ðŸŽ¯ Board members most relevant:**
- **v_Ben**: "Have you actually talked to these people? What did they say?"
- **v_Jeff**: "Would any of these customers be heartbroken if you didn't build this?"
- **v_Meg**: "Is this list representative enough to build a go-to-market around?"

### Step 10: Define Your Core

**Purpose:** Identify the single most important thing your venture does better than anyone else â€” your core differentiator.

**What you produce:** A clear, concise statement of your core â€” the capability, asset, or insight that gives you a unique and defensible advantage.

**How to do it:**
- Your core is NOT a feature list. It's the underlying capability or insight that makes your product uniquely valuable.
- Examples of cores: proprietary data set, unique algorithm, exclusive relationships, domain expertise that takes years to build, network effects, regulatory approval.
- Test it: If a well-funded competitor started today, could they replicate your core within 18 months? If yes, it's not your core â€” it's a feature.
- Your core should be something that gets stronger over time, not weaker. Network effects, data accumulation, and ecosystem lock-in strengthen. Technical features get copied.

**Aulet's "Core" categories:**
1. **Network effects**: Product gets better as more people use it
2. **Customer service excellence**: Deep, trusted relationships that can't be poached
3. **Lowest cost through process innovation**: Structural cost advantage, not just efficiency
4. **User experience**: Design and UX so far ahead that copying the features doesn't copy the experience

**ðŸ”„ NABC Checkpoint:** Your core maps directly to the Competition section of NABC. Run an NABC review: Can you articulate why your core makes your approach superior to ALL alternatives, including the status quo? If not, your core needs strengthening.

**Common mistakes:**
- Confusing features with core ("we have feature X" vs. "we have a data network effect")
- Claiming multiple cores (you can have supporting advantages, but there's one core)
- Picking a core that doesn't align with what the persona values
- Choosing a core that's easily replicable

**ðŸŽ¯ Board members most relevant:**
- **v_Warren**: "Where's the moat? Is this a durable competitive advantage?"
- **v_SunTzu**: "What terrain are you fighting on? Is this ground you can hold?"
- **v_Andy**: "Will this core survive the next strategic inflection point?"

### Step 11: Chart Your Competitive Position

**Purpose:** Map your position against all alternatives â€” direct competitors, indirect competitors, and the status quo â€” on the dimensions your persona cares about most.

**What you produce:** A competitive positioning chart (typically a 2x2 matrix) and a narrative explaining your position.

**How to do it:**
- Select the two dimensions that matter most to your persona (from Steps 5 and 8).
- Plot all alternatives on this 2x2 matrix: direct competitors, indirect solutions, and "do nothing" / status quo.
- Your product should occupy a distinct, valuable position â€” ideally in a quadrant that no competitor currently owns.
- If you're clustered with competitors, either your differentiation isn't strong enough or you're using the wrong axes.

**Important nuance:** The axes must reflect what the CUSTOMER values, not what you think is important. If you choose axes where you look good but customers don't care about those dimensions, the chart is misleading.

**What to include in the competitive analysis:**
- Direct competitors and their strengths/weaknesses

## Theme 4: How Do You Make Money Off Your Product?

### Step 15: Design a Business Model

### Step 16: Set Your Pricing Framework

**Purpose:** Determine what to charge, based on the value you create for the customer (not on your costs).

**What you produce:** A pricing strategy with specific price points, rationale, and supporting evidence.

**How to do it:**
- **Value-based pricing**: Price is determined by the value the customer receives, not by your cost to deliver. If your product saves the persona $50,000/year, charging $5,000/year is a 10:1 value ratio â€” highly attractive.
- Research: What does the persona currently spend on solving this problem (or living with it)? What's their budget for this category? What do alternatives cost?
- Test pricing with real conversations. Ask customers: "If this product delivered [value], what would you be willing to pay?" and "At $X/month, would this be a no-brainer, a maybe, or a pass?"

**Pricing principles:**
- **Price to value, not to cost**: Your costs are irrelevant to the customer. Their willingness to pay comes from perceived value.
- **The 10:1 rule**: Aim for the customer to receive 10x the value of what they pay. This makes the purchase decision easy.
- **Anchor high, offer value**: It's easier to lower prices than raise them.
- **Simplicity**: If the customer can't understand your pricing in 30 seconds, it's too complex.

**Common mistakes:**
- Cost-plus pricing (adding a margin to your costs)
- Pricing by analogy with competitors without understanding value difference
- Offering too many tiers too early
- Not testing pricing with real customers

### Step 18: Design a Scalable Revenue Engine

**Purpose:** Design a repeatable, scalable system for acquiring customers and generating revenue â€” one that grows without requiring proportional increases in resources and doesn't depend on the founders doing every sale.

**What you produce:** A scalable revenue engine blueprint showing how customers are acquired, activated, retained, and expanded â€” with unit economics (CAC, LTV:CAC ratio) and a clear path to scale.

**Why "Scalable Revenue Engine" and not just "Sales Process":**

A sales process is a sequence of steps. A revenue engine is a SYSTEM. The difference matters enormously, especially for platform businesses, marketplaces, and scalable technology ventures:
- A sales process answers: "How do we close this deal?"
- A revenue engine answers: "How does the business generate revenue predictably, repeatedly, and at increasing efficiency as it grows?"

For traditional enterprise sales, the revenue engine might be a well-oiled direct sales machine. But for platforms, marketplaces, SaaS, and other scalable models, the revenue engine often involves fundamentally different mechanisms.

**How to do it:**

**Part 1: Map the Revenue Engine Architecture**

Identify which type(s) of revenue engine your business requires:

**Self-Service / Product-Led Growth (PLG):**
- Customers find, try, adopt, and pay with minimal human interaction
- Revenue scales through product experience, not headcount
- Key metrics: activation rate, free-to-paid conversion, expansion revenue
- Examples: Slack, Dropbox, Notion, Figma
- Critical design question: "What is the product experience that makes users convert and expand without a salesperson?"

**Viral / Network-Effect Engine:**
- Existing users bring new users through usage, sharing, or inherent product dynamics
- Revenue scales through user behavior, not marketing spend
- Key metrics: viral coefficient (K-factor), cycle time, network density
- Examples: WhatsApp, LinkedIn, Airbnb, Uber
- Critical design question: "How does each new user make the product more valuable for existing users AND attract additional users?"

**Platform / Marketplace Engine:**
- Value comes from connecting two or more sides (buyers/sellers, creators/consumers, supply/demand)
- Revenue scales through liquidity â€” the more participants, the more transactions
- Key metrics: liquidity (matches per search), take rate, GMV, supply/demand balance
- Examples: Amazon Marketplace, Uber, Airbnb, App Store
- Critical design question: "How do we solve the chicken-and-egg problem? Which side do we subsidize first? How do we achieve liquidity in our beachhead?"

**Content / SEO / Inbound Engine:**
- Content attracts prospects organically; the content library becomes a compounding asset
- Revenue scales through content leverage, not proportional spending
- Key metrics: organic traffic, content-to-lead conversion, CAC trend over time
- Critical design question: "What content strategy creates a compounding asset that lowers CAC over time?"

**Direct Sales Engine:**
- Human salespeople manage relationships and close deals
- Can scale, but requires proportional investment in people
- Key metrics: quota attainment, sales cycle length, CAC, revenue per rep
- Critical design question: "Can we make this sales process repeatable enough that a new hire reaches productivity in weeks, not months?"

**Channel / Partnership Engine:**
- Revenue comes through partners who sell, integrate, or distribute your product
- Scales through partner leverage, but requires margin sharing
- Key metrics: partner activation rate, partner-sourced revenue %, channel margin
- Critical design question: "What value do partners get from selling us? Is it enough to make us a priority, not an afterthought?"

**Most scalable businesses combine multiple engines.** Slack started with viral/PLG, then added direct sales for enterprise. Airbnb combined marketplace dynamics with SEO/content.

**Part 2: Design for Scale â€” The Key Questions**

Regardless of engine type, answer these questions:

1. **Acquisition**: How do new customers discover you? Is this channel scalable, or does it saturate?
2. **Activation**: What's the "aha moment" â€” when does a new user first experience the core value? How quickly does this happen?
3. **Retention**: Why do customers stay? What creates switching costs or habit?
4. **Revenue**: When and how do customers pay? Is payment aligned with value delivery?
5. **Referral**: Do customers bring other customers? What triggers this?
6. **Expansion**: Do customers increase spending over time? How â€” more users, more features, more usage?

For each: What's the current metric? What's the target? What's the bottleneck?

**Part 3: Unit Economics**

Calculate and validate:

- **Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC)**: Total sales + marketing cost / new customers acquired
  - Include ALL costs: salaries, tools, ad spend, content production, partner commissions, overhead
  - Calculate separately for each acquisition channel
  - Track trend over time (should decrease as engine matures)

- **LTV:CAC Ratio**: From Step 17's LTV calculation
  - **LTV:CAC > 3:1**: Healthy â€” invest in growth
  - **LTV:CAC = 1:1 to 3:1**: Optimize before scaling
  - **LTV:CAC < 1:1**: Every customer loses money â€” fix or pivot

- **Payback Period**: How many months until a customer's revenue covers their CAC?
  - < 12 months: Good
  - 12â€“18 months: Acceptable for high-LTV businesses
  - > 18 months: Dangerous unless you have significant capital reserves

- **Marginal Economics**: What happens to CAC as you scale?
  - Does it decrease? (Network effects, SEO compounding, viral loops) â†’ Scalable âœ…
  - Does it stay flat? (Paid acquisition at consistent rates) â†’ Scalable with capital âš ï¸
  - Does it increase? (Saturating channels, moving to harder-to-reach segments) â†’ Scaling problem ðŸš¨

**Part 4: Platform-Specific Revenue Engine Design**

For platform and marketplace models (which you frequently work on), additional design elements:

**Solving the Cold Start Problem:**
- Which side do you acquire first? (Usually supply â€” it's more patient)
- How do you provide value to early users BEFORE network effects kick in? (Single-player mode)
- What's your beachhead geography or niche where you can achieve liquidity first?
- Can you seed one side of the marketplace manually? (Airbnb famously photographed listings themselves)

**Achieving Liquidity:**
- What's the minimum viable liquidity? (Enough supply that demand finds what they want; enough demand that supply gets transactions)
- How do you measure liquidity? (Search-to-fill rate, time-to-match, utilization rate)
- At what point do network effects become self-reinforcing?

**Monetization Timing:**
- When in the platform's lifecycle do you start charging? (Too early kills growth; too late trains users to expect free)
- Which side pays? (Usually the side that gets more value or has more willingness to pay)
- What's the take rate? (Industry benchmarks: marketplaces 10â€“25%, app stores 15â€“30%, payment platforms 2â€“3%)

**Defensibility Through Network Effects:**
- Same-side effects: Does more supply attract more supply? (e.g., developers on a platform)
- Cross-side effects: Does more supply attract more demand and vice versa?
- Data network effects: Does more usage improve the product? (e.g., AI recommendation quality)
- At what scale do network effects create a meaningful moat?

**ðŸ”„ NABC Checkpoint:** With the full revenue engine designed and unit economics calculated, run an NABC focused on Benefits per Costs â€” but now from YOUR business perspective as well as the customer's. Key questions:
- Is the business model sustainable at scale?
- Is the value you capture sufficient relative to the cost of creating and delivering it?
- Does the revenue engine design create competitive advantages (network effects, compounding content, ecosystem lock-in)?
- For platforms: Does the revenue engine solve the chicken-and-egg problem convincingly?

**Common mistakes:**
- Designing for the end state without a plan for how to get there (the cold start problem)
- Assuming viral growth without designing the viral loop
- Underestimating the true cost of sales (forgetting overhead, tools, management time)
- Assuming online/inbound acquisition when the product actually requires consultative sales
- Not tracking conversion rates at each stage of the funnel
- For platforms: Trying to monetize before achieving liquidity
- For platforms: Not understanding which side to subsidize and for how long

## Using DE for Existing Business Ideas (Not Just Startups)

While Disciplined Entrepreneurship was designed for new ventures, the framework is equally valuable for evaluating and strengthening existing business ideas, products, or strategic initiatives. Here's how to adapt it:

**For existing products seeking new markets:**
- Start at Step 1 (Market Segmentation) to identify new beachheads
- Use Steps 3â€“5 to build personas for the new market
- Steps 8 and 11 help you understand if your value proposition translates

**For products that aren't gaining traction:**
- Step 19 (Identify Key Assumptions) is your diagnostic starting point
- Step 22 ("Dogs Eat Dog Food") reveals whether you have product-market fit
- Steps 12â€“13 expose whether your acquisition process is broken

**For evaluating competitive threats:**
- Steps 10â€“11 (Core and Competitive Position) help you assess your defensibility
- Step 19 identifies which assumptions a competitor could invalidate

**For pivoting or expanding:**
- Step 14 (Follow-on Markets) reveals expansion opportunities
- Step 2 criteria help evaluate whether a new direction is a better beachhead
- Full NABC review tests whether the pivot improves the value proposition

**For preparing a pitch or funding round:**
- Step 24 (Business Plan) synthesizes everything
- Run the final NABC checkpoint to create a compelling executive summary
- Steps 4 and 14 provide the market sizing investors expect
